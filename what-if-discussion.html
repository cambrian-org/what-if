But... why do we care about eyes...?

I think the question that we should instead ask is:

Can we computationally discover and innovate like nature has over the last 3.5 billion years?

One way to do it would be mimic, or copy, how nature has evolved eyes over the last 3.5 billion years. If we can replicate the process computationally and have the right computational models of biological phenomena, then maybe we can also computationally discover new things. Vision is exciting because natural vision has far outpaced artificial vision in not only performance and robustness, but also in the diversity of solutions found under the constraints that earth has provided.

By having the sufficiently plausible computational models for biological phenomena and mimicking the process of discovery, we can study science on a large scale and invent more intelligent visual machines. Our framework opens up exciting possibilities for both science and engineering. For biologists and cognitive scientists, it offers a way to test different hypotheses—like separating the role of optics from neural processing or understanding how environmental pressures shape eye evolution. What’s especially fascinating about computational evolution is that it doesn’t just replicate known pathways; it even stumbled upon compound-eye architectures on its own, without being explicitly designed to do so. For engineers, these evolutionary simulations provide valuable insights into building artificial visual machines that balance performance with real-world constraints like energy efficiency and manufacturability. Moving forward, we could push this even further by adding more environmental factors or combining evolutionary strategies with machine learning to accelerate the discovery of even more efficient and creative solutions.

The tree of life: Can we have a Cambrian Explosion of Artificial Vision? Perhaps the tree of artificial life would start from a single photoreceptor and single neuron looking out in the world.
